## 1. INTRODUCTION TO MCX

### 1.1 About Multi Commodity Exchange

**Establishment:** October 10, 1972 (as a mutual exchange for cotton traders)

**Modern Evolution:**
- 2003: Demutualized and recognized as modern commodity exchange
- 2004: Full trading in derivatives on commodities
- 2006: Precious metals derivatives launched
- 2012: Expansion to international commodity derivatives
- 2020: Introduction of options on commodities
- 2026: AI-driven contract management

**Current Market Position:**
- India's leading commodity derivatives exchange
- Multi-asset class platform (metals, energy, agri commodities)
- Global participant participation (London, Dubai, Singapore)
- Largest in Asian commodity derivatives market

### 1.2 Regulatory Framework

**Primary Regulator:** SEBI (as of 2016)

**Previous Regulation:** Ministry of Consumer Affairs (till 2016)

**Key Regulatory Acts:**
- Forward Contracts (Regulation) Act, 1952
- Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992
- Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002
- Commodities (Regulation) Amendment Bill, 2021

## 3. TRADING MECHANISM

### 3.1 Trading Platform

**MCX Trading System (MTS):**
- Electronic order matching
- Real-time price discovery
- Order types: Market, Limit, Stop-Loss, IOC, GTD, GTC
- Broker terminal connectivity
- Mobile and web trading interfaces

**Trading Statistics (as of 2026):**
- Daily turnover: ₹2,500+ crore
- Active contracts: 50+
- Registered market participants: 600+ traders and hedgers
- Contracts traded: 10-50 million per day

### 3.2 Trading Hours

**Regular Trading Hours:**
- **Morning Session:** 10:00 AM - 11:30 AM
- **Afternoon Session:** 3:30 PM - 11:30 PM (or as per commodity)
- **Special hours:** Pre-opening 30 minutes before session start
- **Extended hours:** For active contracts during volatile periods

**Contract Expiry Timings:**
- On-the-last-day expiry: Usually 4:00 PM last trading time
- Delivery commencement: Post-expiry
- Notice period: 7-14 days before delivery commencement

### 3.3 Order Types and Execution

**Available Orders:**
1. **Market Order** - Immediate execution at best available price
2. **Limit Order** - Execute at specified price or better
3. **Stop-Loss Order** - Triggered at specified price, then market order
4. **Good-Till-Day (GTD)** - Valid for single trading day
5. **Good-Till-Cancel (GTC)** - Remain active till manual cancellation (max 30 days)
6. **Immediate-or-Cancel (IOC)** - Execute available quantity, cancel remainder
7. **All-or-Nothing (AON)** - Execute entire quantity or cancel

**Price Bands:**
- Typically ±3-5% from previous close
- Volatility-adjusted bands during extreme moves
- Circuit breaker at 20% movement (similar to equity)

## 4. SETTLEMENT AND DELIVERY

### 4.1 Settlement Types

**Cash Settlement:**
- Applicable to: Crude oil, natural gas, certain spices
- Final settlement at expiry at settlement price
- No physical delivery required
- Used for pure financial hedging/speculation

**Physical Delivery:**
- Applicable to: Precious metals, base metals, agricultural commodities
- Seller obligation to deliver after contract expiry
- Buyer takes physical possession through warehouses
- Quality and quantity verification required

### 4.2 Delivery Process

**Steps for Physical Delivery:**

1. **Delivery Notice Period:**
   - Typically 7-14 days before contract expiry
   - Notices posted by clearinghouse
   - Seller intimates delivery intention

2. **Quality Inspection:**
   - Pre-delivery inspection at MCX-approved warehouses
   - Third-party inspectors verify quality/quantity
   - Certificate of analysis issued

3. **Warehouse Receipt:**
   - Upon inspection, warehouse issues receipt
   - Security token generated for electronic settlement
   - Non-transferable except through exchange

4. **Delivery Completion:**
   - Funds transferred through RTGS
   - Warehouse receipt transferred to buyer
   - Buyer can take physical possession or sell in market

### 4.3 Margins and Deposits

**Initial Margin (IM):**
- Typical range: 5-15% of contract value
- Varies by commodity volatility
- Precious metals: 5-7% (lower volatility)
- Agricultural: 10-15% (higher volatility)
- Energy: 8-12% (geopolitical sensitive)

**Maintenance Margin (MM):**
- Typically 75-80% of initial margin
- Margin call if positions fall below MM
- Real-time monitoring and collection

**Mark-to-Market (M2M):**
- Daily settlement at 4:00 PM
- Profits credited, losses debited
- Settlement through RTGS next day

### 4.4 Delivery Centers and Warehouses

**Approved Warehouse Networks:**
- For metals: Commerz Metals, SHCL, MMTC warehouses
- For agri: FCI-approved warehouses, farmers' storage facilities
- For energy: Tank storage facilities (IOC, HPCL, BPCL)
- Geographic distribution for logistics efficiency

## 5. REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

### 5.1 SEBI Oversight

**Commodity Derivatives Regulation:**
- Part IIIA of SEBI Act, 1992
- Unified regulatory framework (post-2016)
- Position limits to prevent monopolistic practices
- Surveillance for market abuse

**Position Limit Framework:**
- Normal position limit: Based on open interest (typically 5-10%)
- Hedge position limit: Higher limits for bona fide hedgers
- Near-month limit: Tighter limits as expiry approaches
- Trader category-wise limits

**Trade Reporting Requirements:**

## 7. COMMODITY DERIVATIVES STRATEGY

### 7.1 Hedging Strategies

**Producer Hedging (e.g., Gold smith):**
- Sell gold futures to lock in prices
- Protects against price decline
- Example: Jeweler sells 1 MT gold futures at ₹6,500/gm
- If prices fall to ₹6,200/gm, futures profit offsets loss

**Consumer Hedging (e.g., Oil refinery):**
- Buy crude oil futures to lock in costs
- Protects against price rise
- Example: Refinery buys crude @ 100 USD/bbl
- If prices rise to 110 USD/bbl, futures profit offsets cost increase

**Basis Trading:**
- Simultaneously buy spot and sell futures (or vice versa)
- Lock in basis spread
- Low risk, low return strategy
- Used by dealers and merchants

### 7.2 Speculation Strategies

**Trend Following:**
- Buy if uptrend, sell if downtrend
- Use technical analysis (moving averages, RSI, MACD)
- Stop-loss orders for risk management
- Take profit at resistance levels

**Mean Reversion:**
- Identify overbought/oversold conditions
- Buy when prices are unusually low
- Sell when prices are unusually high
- Requires deep understanding of fair value

**Seasonal Patterns:**
- Agricultural commodities follow harvest seasons
- Metals show seasonal demand patterns
- Energy follows weather patterns
- Options strategies based on seasonal volatility

### 7.3 Spread Trading

**Calendar Spread:**
- Simultaneously buy and sell different expiry contracts
- Example: Buy March gold, sell April gold
- Profit from price differential convergence

**Inter-Commodity Spread:**
- Trade relationships between related commodities
- Example: Gold-Silver ratio trading
- Based on historical correlations
