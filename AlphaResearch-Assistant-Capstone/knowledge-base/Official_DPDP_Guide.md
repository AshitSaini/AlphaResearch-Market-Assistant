## What is DPDP?

DPDP refers to India's Digital Personal Data Protection framework, primarily the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 and the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025.

The DPDP Act applies to processing of digital personal data in India and, in certain cases, outside India where processing relates to offering goods or services to Data Principals in India.

In a broking context, DPDP is relevant because brokers process large volumes of client personal data including KYC documents, PAN, Aadhaar-linked details, bank information, contact information, demat and trading account data, income details, risk profiles, nominee information, order/trade records, grievances, and digital logs.

**Sources:**
- India Code: The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023.
- MeitY: Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025 and explanatory materials.

## Key DPDP Roles

### Data Principal

The individual to whom personal data relates. For a broker this is usually the client, prospective client, authorised signatory, nominee, guardian, or other individual whose data is processed.

### Data Fiduciary

The entity that determines the purpose and means of processing personal data. A broker will typically act as a Data Fiduciary for client onboarding, KYC, account servicing, trading, reporting, grievance handling, and compliance records.

### Data Processor

An entity that processes personal data on behalf of the Data Fiduciary. Examples may include KYC vendors, cloud service providers, CRM platforms, document storage vendors, analytics systems, back-office vendors, and communication providers.

## Lawful Basis and Consent

Under DPDP, personal data should be processed for lawful purposes and normally based on consent or other permitted legitimate uses.

Broker controls should include:

- Give clients a clear notice before or at the time of collecting personal data.
- Explain what personal data is collected.
- Explain the specific purpose of processing.
- Use plain language that a client can understand.
- Provide a mechanism for consent withdrawal where applicable.
- Maintain evidence of consent or permitted processing basis.
- Avoid processing personal data beyond the disclosed purpose.

## Notice Requirements

The DPDP Rules, 2025 explanatory material states that notice by a Data Fiduciary should be clear, standalone, understandable, and distinct from other information.

For a broking platform, notice should cover:

- KYC data collected.
- Bank and demat details collected.
- Trading and transaction data processed.
- Regulatory reporting and compliance uses.
- Use of processors or service providers.
- Client rights and grievance channels.
- Method to withdraw consent where applicable.
- How to contact the broker for privacy matters.

## Obligations of Brokers as Data Fiduciaries

A broker acting as Data Fiduciary should establish controls for:

- Purpose limitation: process client data only for specified and lawful purposes.
- Data minimisation: collect only data required for onboarding, trading, compliance, reporting, risk, and service delivery.
- Accuracy: take reasonable steps to keep client information accurate and updated.
- Security safeguards: protect personal data from breach, unauthorised access, misuse, alteration, disclosure, or destruction.
- Processor governance: ensure vendors and processors handle data under appropriate contractual and security controls.
- Retention control: retain personal data only as required for the purpose, regulatory obligations, limitation periods, or lawful record-keeping.
- Breach response: identify, assess, contain, and notify relevant authorities / affected Data Principals as required.
- Grievance handling: provide a mechanism for privacy-related client requests and complaints.

## Children and Verifiable Consent

Where personal data of children is processed, DPDP imposes additional care and verifiable consent requirements.

For broking operations:

- Minor accounts, guardian-linked accounts, nominee records, or family account structures may involve children’s data.
- Obtain verifiable consent from parent or lawful guardian where required.
- Avoid profiling, behavioural monitoring, or targeted processing involving children unless legally permitted.

## Significant Data Fiduciary Considerations

Certain Data Fiduciaries may be notified as Significant Data Fiduciaries based on factors such as volume/sensitivity of data, risk of harm, public order, or other government-notified considerations.

If a broker or broking platform is designated or falls within a notified class, additional obligations may include:

- Appointment of a Data Protection Officer.
- Independent data auditor.
- Data Protection Impact Assessment.
- Periodic compliance review.
- Additional measures as prescribed by rules.

## Personal Data Breach Handling

Broking entities should have a privacy incident response plan.

Minimum operational controls:

- Detect and classify suspected data breaches.
- Contain access or leakage quickly.
- Preserve logs and evidence.
- Assess affected clients, data categories, and risk.
- Notify the Data Protection Board and affected Data Principals as applicable.
- Document remediation and preventive controls.

## Broker-Specific DPDP Checklist

For a broking firm or trading platform:

1. Map all personal data collected during account opening, KYC, trading, settlement, client servicing, and grievance handling.
2. Publish a clear privacy notice aligned to DPDP.
3. Maintain consent records and withdrawal process where applicable.
4. Review KYC vendors, cloud vendors, communication platforms, CRMs, and document storage vendors as Data Processors.
5. Implement access controls for PAN, Aadhaar-linked information, bank details, demat details, income proof, and client documents.
6. Encrypt sensitive client records at rest and in transit.
7. Define retention schedules aligned to SEBI/exchange record-keeping and DPDP storage limitation.
8. Maintain audit logs for access to client personal data.
9. Build breach reporting and escalation SOPs.
10. Train front office, operations, compliance, technology, and support teams on DPDP obligations.
11. Ensure client-facing apps/web portals provide privacy notices, grievance channels, and consent management flows.
12. Review marketing and analytics use of client data for purpose limitation and consent.

## Relationship with SEBI and Exchange Compliance

DPDP does not replace SEBI, exchange, depository, KYC, PMLA, or tax record-keeping obligations. It operates alongside them.

In practice:

- KYC and trading records may need to be retained because securities laws require retention.
- DPDP still requires purpose limitation, security safeguards, transparency, and appropriate handling of client rights.
- If there is a conflict, the DPDP Act contains consistency provisions, and legal advice should be taken for specific retention or disclosure situations.

## Practical Answer for Broking Teams

DPDP requirements mean a broker should treat client data as a regulated asset:

- collect it transparently,
- use it only for lawful and disclosed purposes,
- protect it with strong security,
- govern vendors,
- retain it only as required,
- handle breaches quickly,
- and give clients a privacy grievance / rights mechanism.
