# Official Guide: PMLA / AML-CFT Requirements for Brokers

**Last Curated:** 2026-05-30

## What PMLA Means for Brokers

The Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002 (PMLA) and rules framed under it apply to securities market intermediaries, including stock brokers and sub-brokers registered with SEBI.

For brokers, PMLA means maintaining an AML/CFT framework to prevent misuse of broking accounts, demat-linked activity, client funds, securities transfers, and trading activity for money laundering or terrorist financing.

**Primary source:** SEBI Master Circular dated June 06, 2024 on AML/CFT obligations of securities market intermediaries under PMLA.

## Core Broker Obligations

Broker obligations under AML/CFT and PMLA include:

- establish written AML/CFT policies and procedures,
- identify and verify clients through KYC and Client Due Diligence,
- identify beneficial owners where applicable,
- classify clients based on risk,
- monitor transactions on an ongoing basis,
- identify and report suspicious transactions,
- maintain records of prescribed transactions,
- screen clients against sanctions / prohibited lists,
- appoint responsible compliance officers,
- train staff,
- and keep records available for regulatory inspection.

## Client Due Diligence

Brokers should perform Client Due Diligence before onboarding and throughout the relationship.

Controls include:

- identify the client and verify identity using reliable documents or data,
- identify beneficial owner where applicable,
- understand the nature and purpose of the client relationship,
- collect income, occupation, financial profile, and risk information,
- apply enhanced due diligence for high-risk clients,
- avoid opening or continuing accounts where identity cannot be verified,
- periodically update client information and KYC records.

## Client Risk Categorisation

Brokers should classify clients into appropriate risk categories.

Higher-risk indicators may include:

- unusual fund movement,
- complex or opaque ownership structures,
- politically exposed person exposure,
- inconsistent income and trading activity,
- frequent changes in bank accounts or contact details,
- suspicious third-party payments,
- circular or synchronized trading,
- repeated losses or transactions without commercial logic,
- adverse media or sanctions concerns.

## Transaction Monitoring

Brokers should monitor funds, securities, and trades to detect suspicious patterns.

Examples of red flags:

- trades inconsistent with client profile,
- sudden high-value transactions after dormant activity,
- frequent fund transfers without economic rationale,
- transactions structured to avoid thresholds,
- funds from unrelated third parties,
- unusual off-market transfers or demat movement,
- activity linked to sanctioned or high-risk persons.

## Recordkeeping

PMLA requires intermediaries to maintain records of prescribed transactions and client identity information.

Broker records should include:

- client identification records,
- beneficial ownership details,
- account opening and KYC documents,
- transaction records,
- suspicious transaction analysis,
- alerts and closure rationale,
- client communications,
- reporting records,
- internal approvals and escalation notes.

## Suspicious Transaction Reporting

Where a broker has reasonable grounds to suspect money laundering or terrorist financing, the matter should be escalated and reported as required.

Controls:

- define alert scenarios,
- review alerts by trained compliance staff,
- document investigation notes,
- escalate to the Principal Officer,
- report suspicious transactions to FIU-IND where required,
- maintain confidentiality of reports,
- avoid tipping off the client.

## Principal Officer and Designated Director

Brokers should designate responsible officers for AML/CFT compliance.

- **Principal Officer:** responsible for suspicious transaction reporting and AML coordination.
- **Designated Director:** senior management / board-level responsibility for overall PMLA compliance.

These roles should have adequate authority, access to information, and independence.

## Sanctions and CFT Screening

Brokers should screen clients and relevant parties against applicable sanctions and terrorist financing lists.

Controls:

- screen clients at onboarding,
- rescreen periodically,
- screen high-risk changes and suspicious events,
- block or escalate potential matches under policy,
- maintain evidence of screening and disposition,
- comply with directions under AML/CFT and UAPA-related requirements where applicable.

## What a Broker Must Not Do

A broker should not:

- open accounts without completing required KYC / due diligence,
- ignore beneficial ownership checks,
- permit suspicious third-party funds without controls,
- ignore suspicious trading or fund movement,
- tip off clients about suspicious transaction reporting,
- continue high-risk relationships without enhanced due diligence,
- fail to maintain required records,
- fail to report suspicious transactions where required,
- treat AML as a one-time onboarding exercise only.

## Practical Broker Checklist

1. Maintain a board-approved AML/CFT policy.
2. Perform client due diligence and beneficial ownership checks.
3. Risk-rate clients and apply enhanced due diligence where needed.
4. Monitor funds, securities, trades, and account behaviour.
5. Screen clients against sanctions / prohibited lists.
6. Investigate AML alerts with documented rationale.
7. Report suspicious transactions to FIU-IND as required.
8. Maintain records for regulatory inspection.
9. Appoint a Principal Officer and Designated Director.
10. Train employees and periodically test controls.
